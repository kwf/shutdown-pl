Mentorship

The programming languages community is disproportionately and overwhelmingly non-Black. How can we increase the accessibility for and retention of Black students in the field?

As academics, we often have a greater ability to effect change at the university level than elsewhere. As such, we will focus our discussion of accessibility on undergraduate students (particularly those attending HBCUs and universities without PL faculty), and we will focus our discussion of retention on graduate students already in the field.

We will plan structural changes that need to occur as well as individual action that we can take as allies and mentors to Black students that will allow them to thrive in our community.

Reading List

  • Collectors, Nightlights, and Allies, Oh My. This article specifically addresses how white people can be effective mentors to Black people and is worth reading in its entirety.
  • Navigating racism: Black graduate students need support. Research shows that the concept of “othermothering” and an “ethics of care,” which, broadly speaking, include mentorship beyond strictly academic matters, can be an immensely positive resource for marginalized students. Can we leverage insights from the first article into an ethics of care so that the emotional labor of othermothering does not always fall on Black (most often women) professors? For optional additional reading about othermothering and ethics of care, you can read or skim Othermothering in the Academy: Using Maternal Advocacy for Institutional Change and Effectively Mentoring Women of Color on the College Campus: A Holistic and Intersectional Ecology (HIE) Model.
